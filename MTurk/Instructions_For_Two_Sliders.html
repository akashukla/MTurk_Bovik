Instructions:

The two slider bars under each image control the quality of the image by way of compression. The highest quality value is 50, and the lowest quality value is 0. Moving the sliders to the left will decrease the quality of the image. Each slider should be moved to mark a particular quality point, as discussed below. The first slider will be the only one active until a value is selected and the "next" button is pressed. Then, the first slider will be disabled and the second slider will be activated. After a value is chosen for the second slider, click "next" to proceed to the next image.

Your task: For the first slider bar, adjust the slider and try to find the point right before any distortion becomes noticeable. Distortion is any change that you can see between the resulting and the original images. In other words, find the slider point furthest to the left where the image still looks exactly like the original image. Below are one good example and two bad examples.
In the good example shown below, the slider is moved as far to the left as possible without causing any noticeable changes in the image. Moving the slider to the left will cause a distortion in the resulting image, while moving the slider to any point to the right will result in an image that looks the same as the original.

In this first bad example, the slider is not moved enough to the left. If the slider is at a certain point

and the resulting image's quality is the same as the original image's quality, even after moving the slider a bit more to the left,

then you can still move the slider to the left.
In the second bad example, the slider is moved too far to the left. If the slider is at a certain point and the resulting image's quality looks different than the original image,

then you have moved the slider too far to the left and must move the slider to the right to a higher quality.

As for the second slider, adjust the slider and try to find the point where sliding the bar just begins to compromise the quality of the image. The difference between the first and second sliders is that the resulting image and the original image can look different while having the same quality. The first slider asks you to find the point where the resulting and original images look different, and the second slider asks you to find the point where the difference is so large that the quality of the resulting image is now different from the quality of the original image. Below are one good example and two bad examples.
In the good example shown below, the slider is moved as far to the left as possible without causing a change in image quality. Moving the slider slightly to the left will cause the resulting image's quality to drop, while moving the slider to any point to the right will result in an image that may look different but has the same quality.

In the first bad example, the slider is not moved enough to the left, since the image can be compressed further without changing the quality of the image. If the slider is at a certain point

and the resulting image's quality is similar to the original's quality even after moving the slider a bit more to the left,

then you can keep moving the slider further to the left.
In the second bad example, the slider is moved too far to the left, since there is a large drop in quality in the resulting image when compared to the original. If the slider is at a certain point and the resulting image's quality is substantially worse than the original's quality,

then you have moved the slider too far to the left and must move the slider to the right.

Note: The images may start off with different initial qualities. Because of this, base your answer relative to the original image (i.e. when the slider bar is all the way to the right at 50). With some images, you may need to move the sliders a lot without affecting the quality of images. With others, you may not need to move the sliders too much before noticing a change in quality. Also, your task is not to rate the aesthetic appeal of the image.
